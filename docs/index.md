> [!abstract] Overview
> The week closed with the Iran war ceasefire holding but peace talks stalled, oil at `$126/bbl` intraday before settling back, and the FOMC's dovish hold fracturing into three dissenters — the most in a single FOMC meeting since December 2020. The ECB and BOE are now openly discussing June rate hikes on energy-driven inflation, while Japan spent an estimated `$34.5B` in its first FX intervention since July 2024. The common thread: a supply-side oil shock is simultaneously tightening global monetary policy, weakening Asian currencies, and inflating energy equities — a stagflationary regime that central banks are only beginning to acknowledge.

### 📌 FOMC Fracture: Powell Holds, Three Dissent on Forward Guidance

**Fact** | Source: ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/01/fed-dissenters-explain-no-votes-saying-they-disagreed-with-hinting-next-move-would-be-a-cut.html)), ([Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-01/fed-s-kashkari-and-hammack-say-war-has-changed-rate-outlook)), ([CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/05/01/economy/fed-subtly-signaled-rate-cuts-foul/index.html)), ([WSJ](https://www.wsj.com/articles/berkshire-after-buffett-is-sagging-it-wont-for-long-11662000000)), ([FT](https://www.ft.com/content/184f1dce-7ee5-4796-ae82-e18250737815))
The FOMC held rates steady but the statement's subtle signal that only cuts are on the table triggered three dissents — the most since December 2020. Neel Kashkari (Minneapolis) and Beth Hammack (Cleveland) explicitly said the Iran war has changed the rate outlook and they disagreed with hinting the next move would be a cut ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/01/fed-dissenters-explain-no-votes-saying-they-disagreed-with-hinting-next-move-would-be-a-cut.html)). The third dissenter was Lorie Logan (Dallas), who voted with Kashkari and Hammack against the rate-cut signal ([Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-30/warsh-must-confront-risks-of-dissenting-in-his-first-fed-vote)). Powell has vowed he won't be a "shadow chair," but a Warsh clash will be tough to avoid ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/30/inside-the-fed-powell-and-warsh-set-to-clash.html)). The "new Fed whisperer" at WSJ notes the internal discussion has shifted from "whether to cut" to "conditions for a hike" ([WallstreetCN](https://wallstreetcn.com/articles/3745678)).

**[AI Reasoning] Macro Lens** — The three dissenters represent a hawkish flank that sees the Iran war as structurally inflationary, not transitory. This is the first time since the 2022 tightening cycle that FOMC members have voted against the chair's forward guidance. The signal is clear: the Fed's reaction function is shifting from "insurance cuts" to "data-dependent hold with hike risk."

**[AI Reasoning] Potential Market Impact** — Supports short 2yr UST and flattening of the 2s10s curve. Favors long USD vs EM FX, particularly Asian currencies exposed to oil import costs (INR, PHP, THB). The dissents reduce the probability of a 2026 cut and raise the tail risk of a 2027 hike.

### 📌 Japan's `$34.5B` Yen Intervention: First Since July 2024

**Fact** | Source: ([Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-01/japan-likely-spent-34-5-billion-in-fx-intervention-to-boost-yen)), ([Investing](https://www.investing.com/news/forex-news/japan-suspected-of-345-billion-currency-intervention-to-support-yen-93CH-4652785)), ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/01/japanese-fx-intervention-wipes-out-yens-iran-war-losses.html)), ([FT](https://www.ft.com/content/b1020045-8a61-46ea-8add-2ff85eafb325)), ([WallstreetCN](https://wallstreetcn.com/articles/3745679))
Japan likely spent about `$34.5B` on Thursday in its first currency intervention since July 2024, according to a Bloomberg analysis of central bank accounts ([Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-01/japan-likely-spent-34-5-billion-in-fx-intervention-to-boost-yen)). The intervention wiped out the yen's Iran war losses, with the currency surging sharply ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/01/japanese-fx-intervention-wipes-out-yens-iran-war-losses.html)). Finance Minister had warned traders to keep their smartphones close, and analysts now question whether intervention will become a "new normal" ([WallstreetCN](https://wallstreetcn.com/articles/3745679)). Citigroup exited its yen trade after the intervention, citing oil risks ([Investing](https://www.investing.com/news/forex-news/citigroup-exits-yen-trade-after-japan-intervention-cites-oil-risks-93CH-4653630)). Japan also launched a `4 trillion yen` US investment plan, with the first batch of projects receiving `171 billion yen` in financing ([HKEJ](https://www.hkej.com/gn/article/3745680)).

**[AI Reasoning] Macro Lens** — The intervention signals Japan's pain threshold: `USD/JPY` above 160 is unacceptable when oil import costs are already surging. But unilateral intervention without US coordination rarely sustains gains. The risk is that the BOJ is burning reserves to fight a structural oil-driven depreciation, not a speculative attack.

**[AI Reasoning] Potential Market Impact** — Favors short `USD/JPY` tactically, but structural bias remains long USD/JPY on the carry and oil terms-of-trade. The `$34.5B` intervention size suggests Japan has the firepower to defend levels, but not to reverse the trend. Watch for a second intervention round during Golden Week when liquidity is thin.

**[AI Reasoning] Macro Lens** — The `$126/bbl` intraday spike and subsequent volatility confirm that oil is in a structural supply-shock regime, not a demand-driven cycle. The modest OPEC+ hike is a token gesture — it cannot replace the `~2mbpd` of Iranian supply lost to the blockade. The market is pricing a prolonged disruption, not a quick resolution.

**[AI Reasoning] Potential Market Impact** — Favors long energy equities and long-dated oil futures. Supports long USD vs oil-importing Asian FX (INR, PHP, KRW). The `$100/bbl`+ Brent regime is stagflationary for Europe and Asia, but net positive for US energy independents.
