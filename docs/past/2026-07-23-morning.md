> [!abstract] Overview
> Oil surged in late trading after Yemen’s Houthis struck two Saudi tankers in the Red Sea, pushing Brent near `$95` and deepening fears of a wider Middle East supply disruption as the US threatened to bomb Iranian civilian facilities and surged forces to the region. Gold extended its gains, supported by safe‑haven flows and a softer dollar, while the World Bank’s chief economist warned that an escalating conflict could slash global growth to `1.3%`. Trade‑war frictions broadened with new US tariffs on generic drugs and Brazilian exports, and the 1930 Section 338 power was readied against Canada, though US‑China diplomacy offered a modest counterpoint with plans to form investment and trade boards ahead of Xi’s September visit. The session’s common thread is a war‑driven risk‑premium repricing that simultaneously lifts oil and gold, keeps inflation‑sensitive central banks on edge, and threatens to stall global growth.
