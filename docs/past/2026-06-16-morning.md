> [!abstract] Overview
> The US‑Iran preliminary peace accord dominates global assets, sending oil prices and bond yields sharply lower while equities rally on the prospect of a full Strait of Hormuz reopening as soon as Friday. The rotation is tempered by ECB hawk Joachim Nagel insisting the inflationary impact of the war persists, and by uncertainty over how quickly sanctions on Chinese oil importers will be lifted. Meanwhile, President Trump escalated trade‑war risk with a 100% tariff threat on French wines, and the EU accused China of training Russian troops, adding to geopolitical cross‑currents. A weak Chinese credit print and a stall in US manufacturing output keep the growth outlook fragile. Attention now turns to the BOJ’s widely expected rate hike to 1% and any forward‑guidance signal from policy architect Uchida later today — the yen and JGBs will hinge on whether the hike marks a steady tightening cycle or a one‑off move. The dominant thread is a fragile risk‑on rotation on peace hopes, checked by residual hawkishness, trade bluster, and China‑Europe tensions.

### 📌 US‑Iran deal sends oil, yields plunging; traders await Hormuz reopening details

**Fact**
Oil held its biggest drop in more than two weeks as traders, shippers and producers awaited details of a US‑Iran deal that’s intended to pave the way for the full reopening of the Strait of Hormuz ([BBG 1](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-15/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-june-16)). US President Donald Trump said the Strait of Hormuz could reopen on Friday ([BBG 2](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-15/gold-holds-gain-as-trump-touts-reopening-of-hormuz-this-week)). The market movement followed official announcements that Washington and Tehran had reached a preliminary accord to end their four‑month war. A reported 14‑point Persian‑language draft memorandum of understanding showed the US committing to lift its maritime blockade ([SCMP 1](https://www.scmp.com/economy/global-economy/article/3357186/after-us-iran-accord-and-oil-price-plunge-analysts-warn-grey-area-chinese-firms)). “New purchases of Iranian oil ostensibly wouldn’t be subject to fresh sanctions, but at the same time, I imagine we won’t see a rush to lift existing sanctions on Chinese importers of Iranian oil,” said Lynn Song, chief economist for Greater China at ING ([SCMP 1](https://www.scmp.com/economy/global-economy/article/3357186/after-us-iran-accord-and-oil-price-plunge-analysts-warn-grey-area-chinese-firms)).
