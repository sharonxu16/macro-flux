> [!abstract] Overview
> Oil extended gains after President Trump declared the Iran ceasefire on “life support,” crushing hopes for a near-term reopening of the Strait of Hormuz and driving a broad safe-haven bid into the dollar. The supply shock is now feeding a hawkish pivot across the global rate complex: the Bank of Japan signaled a June hike is on the table, ECB officials warned they may need to act on price stability, and South Korea’s benchmark bond yield topped 4% for the first time in years on mounting rate-hike bets. Adding to the flight-to-safety, the UK gilt market suffered another brutal sell-off as Prime Minister Starmer’s leadership crisis deepened, pushing long-term borrowing costs to their highest since 1998. The cross-market thread is a widening stagflationary impulse—surging energy costs are forcing central banks to abandon easing and contemplate tightening just as geopolitical uncertainty saps risk appetite, pressuring equities, emerging-market currencies, and global bonds.

### 📌 Iran Ceasefire on “Life Support” as Trump Rejects Tehran’s Counterproposal; Wall Street Embraces ‘Nacho’ Trade

**Fact**
Oil prices rose on Tuesday as U.S. President Donald Trump’s comments that the ceasefire with Iran is on “life support” diminished hopes for a near-term peace deal and intensified concerns over a prolonged Middle East conflict that has effectively closed the Strait of Hormuz ([CNBC 1](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/12/oil-prices-today-brent-wti-trump-iran-war-hormuz.html)). Trump told reporters in the White House that Iran’s counterproposal was a “piece of garbage” and that he “didn’t even finish reading it,” describing the ceasefire as being on “massive life support” ([SCMP 1](https://www.scmp.com/plus/news/china/diplomacy/article/3353252/trump-rejects-garbage-iran-peace-plan-heading-china)). “Peace deal hopes fade after Trump rejects ‘garbage’ Iran counterproposal” ([Reuters 1](https://news.google.com/rss/articles/CBMisAFBVV95cUxQYjI0ek5ZYzN4WXRWbDlpVERUd1VlZ3BfVlo3RkpKYTZKRy0zelR1VTc4NUZxc3RVenFGdFJ2QUMxekFuOEM4TkllY1JvQ3dfdEx6Z0lYY1N6Z0VGSWJGdHBiNWVFVDF5YTZlRGdwdzdta2dteDZsZW16Tnh4TExPRXZBZFFTLXFJX1ZydXhqX0RVaVpnS0oxLTR3RDF5dGpvZHgwbkQzbkdtNjJjRTd1aQ)). With a fragile US-Iran ceasefire barely holding and the Strait of Hormuz still blockaded, investors are embracing a new market narrative: “Nacho,” an acronym for “Not a chance Hormuz opens,” reflecting a growing bet on prolonged gridlock and high oil prices ([SCMP 2](https://www.scmp.com/economy/china-economy/article/3353215/taco-nacho-new-buzzword-wall-street-amid-trumps-trip-china)). This marks a sharp pivot from last year’s dominant “Taco” tactic—“Trump always chickens out”—which was born in the chaos of Trump’s tariff blitz ([SCMP 2](https://www.scmp.com/economy/china-economy/article/3353215/taco-nacho-new-buzzword-wall-street-amid-trumps-trip-china)). Emerging-market currencies and stocks fell as hopes for peace dimmed ([BBG 1](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-12/emerging-market-currencies-stocks-fall-as-iran-peace-hopes-dim)). The impact of the war is reverberating through consumer supply chains: Japanese snack maker Calbee is draining color from its potato chip packaging in response to a shortage and rising price of petroleum-based inks, as the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz has severely disrupted global supplies of energy and petrochemicals ([FT 1](https://www.ft.com/content/3b40f017-16c7-4540-874a-58227071f636), [BBC 1](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c78k405j8pdo)). The physical damage to energy infrastructure is long-lasting; a UAE gas plant hit by Iranian attacks will not be fully repaired until 2027, highlighting the lasting impact of the conflict on Gulf energy exports ([FT 2](https://www.ft.com/content/ca7434f9-28f0-4a36-a8c1-1bd2c46c8154)).
